Dental Emergency Phone Scripts: How to Handle Urgent Calls Professionally

Published: March 18, 2026 | Reading Time: 10 minutes

Emergency calls require calm, efficient handling. These scripts help your front desk team triage urgent situations, provide immediate guidance, and get patients the care they need quickly.

Emergency Triage Questions

When a patient calls with a dental emergency, start with these triage questions to assess the situation:

Initial Assessment Script:

"I understand this is urgent. Let me ask a few quick questions to help you best:"

  1. "Are you in severe pain right now?"
  2. "Is there bleeding that won't stop?"
  3. "Has a tooth been knocked out or displaced?"
  4. "Is there significant swelling in your face or jaw?"
  5. "Have you had any trauma to your mouth or face?"

True Emergency Script (Immediate Care Needed)

For situations requiring immediate attention (severe swelling, uncontrolled bleeding, trauma):

True Emergency Response:

Staff: "Based on what you've described, you need immediate attention. Let me get you in right away."

"Can you come to our office at [address] in the next [time frame]?"

"While you're on your way, here's what to do:"

  • "If you're driving, please have someone else drive if possible."
  • "If a tooth was knocked out, keep it moist in milk or saliva."
  • "Apply gentle pressure to any bleeding areas with clean gauze."

"What's the best number to reach you if we need to contact you?"

Urgent Care Script (Same-Day Appointment)

For situations that need same-day attention but aren't life-threatening:

Urgent Care Response:

Staff: "I can hear you're uncomfortable. Let me get you in today to address this."

"I have availability at [time]. Does that work for you?"

"In the meantime, here are some things that might help:"

  • "Over-the-counter pain relievers can help manage discomfort."
  • "Avoid very hot or cold foods and drinks."
  • "If there's swelling, a cold compress on the outside of your cheek can help."

"What phone number should we use to send you a confirmation text?"

After-Hours Emergency Script

For calls that come in outside office hours:

After-Hours Response (Voicemail Greeting):

"Thank you for calling [Practice Name]. Our office is currently closed."

"If you are experiencing a dental emergency, please:"

  1. "Press 1 to be connected to our on-call dentist."
  2. "Or call [emergency number] directly."
  3. "For life-threatening emergencies, please call 911 or go to your nearest emergency room."

"If your concern can wait until our next business day, please leave a message and we'll call you back first thing in the morning."

On-Call Dentist Handoff

If transferring to on-call dentist:

"I'm connecting you with our on-call dentist, Dr. [Name]. They'll be able to help you right away. Please stay on the line."

If leaving message for callback:

"I've noted your emergency and sent a message to Dr. [Name]. They will call you back within [time frame, usually 15-30 minutes]. In the meantime, [provide appropriate comfort measures]."

Knocked-Out Tooth Script

This is one of the most time-sensitive dental emergencies. Every minute counts:

Avulsed Tooth Response:

Staff: "This is time-sensitive - we need to see you immediately. The tooth has the best chance of being saved if we treat it within 30 minutes."

"Here's what to do right now:"

  1. "Find the tooth and pick it up by the crown (the white part), not the root."
  2. "If it's dirty, gently rinse it with milk or water - don't scrub it."
  3. "If possible, gently place it back in the socket and hold it there."
  4. "If you can't put it back, keep it moist in a cup of milk or saliva."
  5. "Come to our office immediately at [address]."

"We're preparing a room for you now. How soon can you get here?"

Severe Pain Script

Severe Pain Response:

Staff: "I'm sorry you're experiencing this level of pain. Let's get you some relief."

"Can you tell me where exactly the pain is? Is it one specific tooth or a broader area?"

"On a scale of 1-10, how would you rate your pain right now?"

"Is the pain constant or does it come and go?"

"Does anything make it better or worse - hot, cold, pressure?"

"I have an appointment available at [time]. Can you come in then, or do you need something sooner?"

Facial Swelling Script

Swelling Response (Potential Infection):

Staff: "Swelling can indicate an infection that needs immediate attention. Let me ask a few important questions:"

  • "How quickly did the swelling develop?"
  • "Do you have any fever or chills?"
  • "Is it painful to open your mouth?"
  • "Do you have any difficulty breathing or swallowing?"

If difficulty breathing/swallowing:

"This could be serious. If you're having any trouble breathing, please go to your nearest emergency room immediately or call 911. I'll note your file and Dr. [Name] will follow up."

If no breathing issues:

"This needs to be seen today. I can get you in at [time]. In the meantime, a cold compress on the outside of your face may help with discomfort."

Broken or Chipped Tooth Script

Broken Tooth Response:

Staff: "I understand you've broken a tooth. Let me get some details so we can help you."

"Is the tooth painful? Is it sensitive to hot or cold?"

"Is there a sharp edge that's cutting your tongue or cheek?"

"If you have the broken piece, please bring it with you. We may be able to reattach it."

"Keep it moist in a small container with milk or water."

"I have availability at [time]. Does that work for you?"

Lost Filling or Crown Script

Lost Restoration Response:

Staff: "A lost filling or crown can be uncomfortable, but we can help you with that."

"Is the tooth sensitive? Any pain?"

"If you still have the crown, please bring it with you - we may be able to recement it."

"In the meantime, avoid chewing on that side and stay away from very hot or cold foods."

"I can get you an appointment on [date/time]. Does that work, or would you like to come in sooner if something opens up?"

Providing Calm Reassurance

Remember that patients calling with emergencies are often anxious or in pain. Use these phrases to provide reassurance:

Reassurance Phrases:

  • "We're going to take good care of you."
  • "You're doing the right thing by calling."
  • "This is exactly what we handle - you're in good hands."
  • "Dr. [Name] sees situations like this regularly and knows exactly what to do."
  • "Let's get you comfortable as quickly as possible."
  • "I understand this is stressful. We'll get through this together."

Emergency Documentation Script

Information to Capture:

"Let me get some information for Dr. [Name]:"

  • "Your full name and date of birth?"
  • "Are you a current patient of our practice?"
  • "What's the best phone number to reach you?"
  • "Can you briefly describe what happened and when it started?"
  • "Are you allergic to any medications?"
  • "Are you currently taking any medications?"

Quick Reference: Emergency Priority Levels

Priority Situation Response
IMMEDIATE Knocked-out tooth, uncontrolled bleeding, difficulty breathing, severe swelling See immediately or direct to ER
SAME-DAY Severe pain, broken tooth, swelling (no breathing issues) Same-day emergency slot
URGENT Lost filling/crown, moderate pain, cracked tooth (no pain) Within 24-48 hours

Key Takeaways

  • Stay calm: Your demeanor sets the tone for anxious patients
  • Ask the right questions: Triage quickly to determine urgency level
  • Provide immediate guidance: Help patients while they wait for their appointment
  • Document everything: Capture information for the dentist's preparation
  • Know when to escalate: Some situations require ER referral, not dental care

Having these scripts readily available helps your team respond professionally and efficiently to any dental emergency, improving patient outcomes and reducing stress for everyone involved.
