How to Calculate Your Dental Practice's Patient Acquisition Cost

Published: March 11, 2026 · Reading Time: 11 minutes

Knowing your Patient Acquisition Cost (PAC) is essential for making informed marketing decisions. Yet most dental practices don't know this critical number. In this guide, you'll learn exactly how to calculate PAC, benchmark it against industry standards, and use it to optimize your marketing investments.

What Is Patient Acquisition Cost?

Definition

Patient Acquisition Cost (PAC) is the total amount you spend on marketing and sales to acquire one new patient. It includes all the costs associated with attracting, converting, and onboarding a new patient to your practice.

Why It Matters

Without knowing your PAC, you can't answer critical questions:

  • Is my marketing profitable or losing money?
  • Which marketing channels are most efficient?
  • How much can I afford to spend to get a new patient?
  • Should I increase or decrease my marketing budget?

The PAC Formula

Basic Formula

PAC = Total Marketing Costs ÷ Number of New Patients Acquired

Detailed Formula

For more accuracy, include all relevant costs:

Total Marketing Costs Include:

  • Advertising spend (Google Ads, Facebook, etc.)
  • Website costs (hosting, maintenance, updates)
  • SEO and content marketing
  • Social media management
  • Print materials (flyers, business cards, mailers)
  • Community events and sponsorships
  • Referral program costs
  • Marketing software/tools
  • Staff time for marketing activities
  • Agency or consultant fees

Step-by-Step Calculation

Step 1: Define Your Time Period

Choose a consistent period for calculation. Monthly or quarterly works best for most practices.

Step 2: Gather Your Costs

Create a comprehensive list of all marketing expenses:

Expense Category Monthly Cost
Google Ads $800
Facebook/Instagram Ads $300
Website hosting & maintenance $100
SEO service $500
Yelp/Directory listings $150
Community sponsorships $200
Referral rewards program $100
Marketing software $50
Staff time (5 hrs @ $20/hr) $100
Total Marketing Costs $2,300

Step 3: Count New Patients

Track how many new patients were acquired during the same period:

  • New patients who completed their first appointment
  • Source: Practice management software reports
  • Be consistent with your definition of "new patient"

Example Practice:

  • Marketing spend: $2,300/month
  • New patients acquired: 18
  • PAC = $2,300 ÷ 18 = $127.78 per new patient

Calculating PAC by Channel

Why Channel-Specific PAC Matters

Different marketing channels have different costs and conversion rates. Understanding channel-specific PAC helps you allocate budget efficiently.

Channel Analysis Example

Channel Spend New Patients PAC
Google Ads $800 8 $100
Facebook Ads $300 2 $150
SEO/Organic $500 3 $167
Yelp/Reviews $150 2 $75
Referrals $100 3 $33
Totals $1,850 18 $103

Key Insights from Channel Analysis

  • Referrals have lowest PAC: Invest more here
  • Google Ads are efficient: Good ROI
  • Facebook Ads have higher PAC: May need optimization
  • SEO has high PAC but long-term value: Consider lifetime impact

What's a "Good" PAC?

A good PAC depends on your Patient Lifetime Value (PLV). General guidelines:

Healthy PAC-to-PLV Ratio:

  • Excellent: PAC is less than 10% of PLV
  • Good: PAC is 10-20% of PLV
  • Acceptable: PAC is 20-30% of PLV
  • Warning: PAC is 30-40% of PLV
  • Problem: PAC exceeds 40% of PLV

Connecting PAC to Patient Lifetime Value

Why LTV Matters

Patient Acquisition Cost only makes sense in context of what a patient is worth over time.

LTV Calculation

LTV = Average Annual Value × Average Patient Tenure

Example LTV Calculation

Factor Value Source
Average production per patient/year $650 Practice software
Average patient tenure 7 years Historical data
Average referrals per patient 1.5 Survey/tracking
Referral conversion rate 40% Tracking

Direct LTV: $650 × 7 years = $4,550

Referral Value: 1.5 referrals × 40% conversion × $4,550 = $2,730

Total LTV: $4,550 + $2,730 = $7,280

The PAC-to-LTV Ratio

Using our example:

  • PAC: $128
  • LTV: $7,280
  • Ratio: $128 ÷ $7,280 = 1.8%

This is excellent! The practice is spending less than 2% of patient value to acquire them.

Optimizing Your PAC

Strategy 1: Improve Conversion Rates

Better conversion means more patients from the same spend:

  • Train staff on phone skills
  • Respond to inquiries faster
  • Follow up with leads consistently
  • Improve your online booking experience

Strategy 2: Focus on High-ROI Channels

Shift budget to channels with lower PAC:

  • Increase referral program investment
  • Double down on profitable ad channels
  • Reduce or eliminate underperforming channels

Strategy 3: Reduce Cost Per Click

For paid advertising:

  • Improve ad quality scores
  • Target more specific keywords
  • Use negative keywords to avoid wasted clicks
  • Optimize landing pages

Strategy 4: Leverage Free Channels

Build presence in low-cost areas:

  • Google Business Profile optimization
  • Online review generation
  • Social media organic content
  • Community involvement

Strategy 5: Capture More from Existing Traffic

Many practices lose leads they've already paid for:

The Hidden PAC Reduction Opportunity:

If you're paying for marketing that drives calls, but missing those calls:

  • You're paying for leads you never capture
  • Your effective PAC is higher than calculated
  • Improving call capture reduces PAC immediately

Example: If 20% of marketing-driven calls are missed, improving call capture could reduce PAC by 20%.

Common PAC Calculation Mistakes

Mistake 1: Undercounting Marketing Costs

Problem: Only counting direct ad spend, ignoring staff time, software, etc.

Result: PAC appears lower than reality

Fix: Include ALL marketing-related expenses

Mistake 2: Misattributing Patient Sources

Problem: Patients say "Google" when they actually saw an ad

Result: Channel PACs are inaccurate

Fix: Use tracking numbers and UTMs for accurate attribution

Mistake 3: Ignoring Time Lag

Problem: Comparing this month's spend to this month's patients

Result: Some patients from last month's marketing aren't counted

Fix: Use 30-60 day attribution windows

Mistake 4: Counting Leads Instead of Patients

Problem: Calculating PAC based on inquiries, not completed appointments

Result: PAC looks great but revenue doesn't match

Fix: Only count patients who complete first appointment

Your PAC Action Plan

Week 1: Establish Baseline

  • Gather all marketing expenses for past 3 months
  • Count new patients acquired during same period
  • Calculate your baseline PAC
  • Estimate your patient LTV

Week 2: Analyze by Channel

  • Set up tracking for each marketing channel
  • Calculate PAC by channel
  • Identify best and worst performing channels

Week 3: Optimize

  • Shift budget toward lower-PAC channels
  • Improve conversion processes
  • Implement call capture for marketing-driven calls

Week 4 and Beyond: Monitor

  • Track PAC monthly
  • Compare to benchmarks
  • Continuously optimize

Summary

Patient Acquisition Cost is a fundamental metric for dental practice growth:

  1. Calculate it: Total marketing spend ÷ new patients
  2. Contextualize it: Compare to patient lifetime value
  3. Break it down: Analyze by channel for insights
  4. Optimize it: Focus on low-PAC channels and improve conversions
  5. Track it: Monitor monthly to ensure marketing profitability

The most successful dental practices know their numbers. Start tracking your PAC today and make data-driven decisions about your marketing investments.
