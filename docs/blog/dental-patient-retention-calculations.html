Dental Patient Retention Calculations: Measuring What Matters

March 11, 2026 9 min read

You can't improve what you don't measure. Understanding patient retention metrics helps you identify problems, track progress, and make informed decisions about practice investments.

Why Retention Metrics Matter

Patient retention is the foundation of practice stability:

  • Retained patients provide predictable revenue
  • Long-term patients have higher lifetime value
  • Retained patients refer more new patients
  • Higher retention means less reliance on expensive marketing
A 5% increase in patient retention can increase profits by 25-95%.

Key Retention Metrics

Patient Retention Rate

The percentage of patients who remain active over a period.

Formula:

Retention Rate = ((Patients at End - New Patients) / Patients at Start) × 100

Example:

  • Start of year: 1,000 active patients
  • New patients during year: 150
  • End of year: 950 active patients
  • Retention Rate = (950 - 150) / 1,000 = 80%

Patient Attrition Rate

The percentage of patients lost over a period.

Formula:

Attrition Rate = (Patients Lost / Patients at Start) × 100

Benchmark: Healthy practices have attrition rates under 10% annually.

Patient Lifetime Value (LTV)

The total revenue a patient generates over their relationship with your practice.

Formula:

LTV = Average Annual Value × Average Years as Patient

Example:

  • Average annual spending: $400
  • Average patient tenure: 7 years
  • LTV = $400 × 7 = $2,800

Recare Compliance Rate

Percentage of patients who schedule and attend regular hygiene visits.

Formula:

Recare Rate = (Patients with Hygiene Visit in 18 months / Active Patients) × 100

Benchmark: 70%+ is considered healthy; 80%+ is excellent.

Understanding the Numbers

What's a Good Retention Rate?

  • Below 70%: Critical concern - significant patient loss
  • 70-80%: Below average - improvement needed
  • 80-85%: Average - acceptable but room to improve
  • 85-90%: Good - healthy practice
  • Above 90%: Excellent - strong patient loyalty

Segmented Analysis

Look at retention by segment to identify issues:

  • By insurance type (cash vs. PPO vs. Medicaid)
  • By age group
  • By treatment type (preventive only vs. restorative)
  • By new patient source (referral vs. marketing vs. insurance)

Calculating Lost Revenue

From Attrition

How much revenue are you losing to patient attrition?

Annual Lost Revenue = Patients Lost × Average Annual Value

Example:

  • 100 patients lost per year
  • Average annual value: $400
  • Annual lost revenue: $40,000
  • 5-year lost value: $200,000 (assuming LTV of $2,000)

From Recare Non-Compliance

Revenue missed from patients not returning for hygiene:

Missed Recare Revenue = (Active Patients × Target Rate - Actual Visits) × Average Hygiene Fee

Retention Improvement ROI

Before investing in retention initiatives, calculate the expected return:

Example: Patient Communication System

  • Current retention: 80%
  • Target retention: 85%
  • Active patients: 1,000
  • Average annual value: $400
  • Additional retained patients: 50
  • Additional annual revenue: $20,000
  • System cost: $5,000/year
  • ROI: 300%

Tracking and Reporting

Monthly Tracking

  • New patients
  • Inactive patients (no visit in 18 months)
  • Reactivate patients
  • Recare appointments scheduled

Quarterly Analysis

  • Retention rate trend
  • Attrition by segment
  • LTV updates
  • Recare compliance rate

Annual Review

  • Year-over-year retention comparison
  • Lifetime value trend
  • Referral rate from retained patients
  • Marketing cost per acquired vs. retained patient

Action Steps

  1. Calculate your current retention rate
  2. Identify your average patient LTV
  3. Set a target retention rate
  4. Identify retention initiatives to reach target
  5. Calculate expected ROI
  6. Implement and track monthly

Conclusion

Retention metrics reveal the health of your practice. By measuring consistently, understanding what the numbers mean, and taking action on insights, you can build a more stable, profitable practice.
