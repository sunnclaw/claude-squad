Dental Practice Phone Etiquette: A Complete Guide

March 11, 2026 11 min read

The phone is often the first contact a potential patient has with your practice. Excellent phone etiquette can convert callers into loyal patients. Poor etiquette can send them to your competitors.

The Business Impact of Phone Skills

Consider these statistics:

  • 85% of callers who reach voicemail hang up without leaving a message
  • 67% of callers won't call back if their call isn't answered
  • Poor phone experiences are the #1 reason new patients choose a different practice
A ringing phone is a revenue opportunity. Every unanswered or poorly handled call is revenue lost.

Answering the Phone

The Ideal Answer

Answer within 3 rings with a warm, professional greeting:

Good: "Thank you for calling [Practice Name]. This is [Your Name]. How may I help you today?"

This greeting:

  • Shows gratitude for the call
  • Identifies the practice clearly
  • Introduces the staff member personally
  • Asks an open question to start the conversation

What to Avoid

  • Answering with just "Hello?"
  • Saying "Hold please" before anything else
  • Sounding rushed or distracted
  • Eating or chewing while on the phone

Key Phone Scenarios

New Patient Inquiry

Script: "I'd be happy to help you schedule an appointment! Are you a new patient to our practice?"

If yes: "Wonderful! Let me get some information so we can schedule your first visit. May I have your name please?"

Scheduling Appointment

Script: "Let me check our availability. [Brief pause] I have openings on [Day] at [Time] or [Alternative Day] at [Time]. Which works better for your schedule?"

Always offer specific times rather than asking "When would you like to come in?" which puts the burden on the caller.

Emergency Call

Script: "I understand you're in pain. Let me help you right away. Can you describe what's happening?"

Then: "Let me check with the doctor. Please hold for just a moment while I see when we can get you in today."

Handling Complaints

Script: "I completely understand your frustration, and I apologize for the inconvenience. Let me see what I can do to make this right for you."

Never argue or become defensive. Listen, acknowledge, and resolve.

Voice and Tone

The "Smile Voice"

Smile while talking on the phone. It changes your tone in ways callers can hear. People can literally hear a smile through the phone.

Speaking Pace

Speak at a moderate pace—slow enough to be understood, fast enough to sound energetic. Avoid rushing, which conveys impatience.

Volume Control

Speak clearly at a moderate volume. Don't shout, but don't mumble. If you're in a noisy environment, apologize and find a quieter space.

Placing Callers on Hold

The Right Way

Script: "May I place you on a brief hold while I [check availability/verify your insurance/retrieve your records]?"

Wait for their answer before putting them on hold.

Hold Time Limits

  • Never leave someone on hold longer than 60 seconds without checking back
  • If it will take longer, offer to call them back
  • Thank them for their patience when you return

Ending the Call

Summarize and Confirm

Script: "Let me confirm your appointment. You're scheduled for [Day] at [Time] with Dr. [Name] for [Reason]. Is that correct?"

Closing Professionally

Script: "We look forward to seeing you on [Day]. If you have any questions before then, please don't hesitate to call. Have a great day!"

Handling Difficult Situations

Angry Caller

  • Let them vent without interrupting
  • Acknowledge their feelings
  • Apologize for their experience (not necessarily admitting fault)
  • Offer a specific solution
  • Follow up to ensure satisfaction

Language Barriers

  • Speak slowly and clearly
  • Use simple language
  • Offer to connect with a staff member who speaks their language
  • Consider translation services for critical information

Unsure of Answer

Script: "That's a great question. Let me find out the correct answer for you rather than guessing. May I call you back within [timeframe]?"

Training and Quality Assurance

Regular Training

  • Role-play common scenarios
  • Record and review sample calls (with permission)
  • Provide feedback and coaching
  • Update scripts based on common questions

Quality Metrics

  • Call answer rate
  • Average hold time
  • Call abandonment rate
  • Patient satisfaction scores
  • Conversion rate (inquiries to appointments)

Conclusion

Phone etiquette is a skill that improves with practice and intention. Every call is an opportunity to create a positive impression and build patient loyalty. Invest in training, create scripts for common situations, and continuously improve based on feedback.
